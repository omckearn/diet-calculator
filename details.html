How the scores are assembled, in plain English.

Quick summary

  • The Health Score reflects how closely your habits match recommended dietary patterns.
  • The Environment Score reflects the estimated footprint of those foods.
  • Several important factors (like sodium, food waste, and animal welfare) are not included.

The Scoring Algorithm (and Items Not Included)

What the Health Score Measures

The Diet-Health-Environment Calculator provides a good gauge of a person’s diet’s impact on their health and the environment. It is adapted from a 13-item Rapid Prime Diet Quality Score Screener (rPDQS) developed by Selma Kronsteiner-Gicevic and her colleagues and validated against the Healthy Eating Index.

Our calculator is based on only 16 dietary habits and cannot cover every detail about every individual’s diet. Restaurant meals are particularly challenging because they vary from healthy salads to voluminous, fatty, salty meals with few vegetables or fruit at table-service eateries. Restaurant scores are based on estimated saturated-fat content. Also tough to evaluate are stews, casseroles, and other mixed dishes in which the ingredients could vary tremendously; the algorithm assigns the typical ingredients and nutrients to the various food categories. For restaurant meals and mixed dishes, users of the algorithm could adjust the numbers of servings they consume based on their estimates of the amounts of key ingredients.

Low-fat or fat-free dairy foods are lower in saturated fat and healthier for consumers than their full-fat versions. However, low-fat yogurt and cheese are fermented and may be less beneficial than once thought. Still, milkfat adds calories unneeded by most people and does not provide any benefits. The health benefit of low-fat dairy products to society at large is minimal because the removed milkfat will be consumed in the form of butter, ice cream, or other foods.

What the Environment Score Measures

Environmental scores, not included in the rPDQS, are based on per-kilogram amounts of food and the associated amounts of greenhouse gas emissions (35%), stress-weighted water use (25%), eutrophying emissions (15%), land use (15%), and acidifying emissions (10%). The best way to get a good Environment score is to eat less (or no!) food, but doing that could mean eating fewer healthy foods, thereby holding down the Health score.

What This Calculator Does Not Include

The following factors are not included in the scoring algorithm and deserve a few words of explanation.

  • Salt/sodium: Diets high in sodium cause as many as 100,000 premature deaths annually, but because salt is used so widely throughout the food supply, with amounts varying from brand to brand and with people adding salt to some meals, it is impossible to estimate accurately how much sodium people are consuming. Two major sources of sodium: bread, pizza crust, tortillas (because most people eat many grain products in a day) and table-service restaurant meals (because portions are often huge, highly salted, and contain cheese, processed meat, and dressings and sauces that are high in sodium).
  • Organic foods: Organically produced vegetables, meat, eggs, and other foods are a great benefit to farmers and farm workers because they minimize exposure to synthetic pesticides and antibiotics. Consumers may benefit from lower residues of pesticides in their food and fewer antibiotic-resistant bacteria. The environmental impacts are mixed. Insecticides may kill beneficial insects and reduce the food supply available for birds and other animals, and herbicides may reduce weed populations that otherwise provide food and shelter for insects, birds, and small mammals. However, crop yields are generally lower for organic crops, meaning that it takes more land to produce the same amount of food (including organic meat, which typically comes from animals that consumed organically raised crops).
  • Animal welfare: Pigs raised outside of cages, broiler chickens not confined to huge windowless warehouses, layer hens not crammed into tiny cages, and cattle raised on pastureland for their entire lives instead of being “finished” at feedlots provides major benefits to the animals. Note, though, that grass-fed cattle take longer to mature and therefore emit larger quantities of methane, a potent greenhouse gas, than feedlot cattle. Also, because of their greater need for pastureland, grass-fed (and regeneratively raised) cattle—especially with their higher cost, tougher meat—will inevitably comprise only a small fraction of the beef Americans consume.
  • Food waste: Roughly one-third of all food produced is wasted. That includes crops left on farms; spoilage at packinghouses, factories, and supermarkets; and uneaten food in homes. And that includes perfectly healthful and edible produce that is discarded because of blemishes and packaged foods that passed their “best by” dates even though they were still perfectly edible. Reducing food waste could reduce the amount of food that needs to be produced and the food and packaging dumped in landfills. Everyone could help reduce food waste.
  • Genetically modified (GE) ingredients: Countless packaged foods contain ingredients derived from wheat, corn, and soy crops grown from seeds that were modified to resist the effects of herbicides or to contain a harmless insecticide. Hawaiian papayas have been modified to resist a crop-killing virus, and tiny percentages of potatoes and apples have been modified to resist browning when sliced. The oils, starches, and other common ingredients made from GE crops have been so purified that they do not contain any modified genetic material. There is no evidence that foods containing ingredients from GE crops have harmed anyone’s health though some evidence indicates that glyphosate, the key ingredient in Roundup herbicide used on GE corn, soybeans, canola, and sugar beets, is carcinogenic. GE crops have both positive (e.g., reduced use of toxic pesticides) and negative (e.g., herbicide-resistant weeds and insecticide-resistant insects) environmental consequences.

On all those issues, people can “vote” their preferences when they shop. People can also support local, state, and federal legislation and vote for representatives that support reducing pesticide/fertilizer use, improving animal welfare, improving food labeling, and other measures.
